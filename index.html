Love, But On Purpose

You don't need more love.
You need more intentional moments.

A small space to turn the people you care about into the moments you actually share. One tiny, on-purpose act of love at a time.

The quiet premise

We care about the people in our lives. We just rarely turn that care into something they can feel.

Between lectures, late shifts, and the slow drift of shared group chats, love becomes something we feel — rarely something we do.

This is a small space to change that. Not a tracker. Not a streak. Just a weekly invitation to make one specific, intentional moment of connection — and to notice what happens when you do.
The why behind this

Because care, unspoken, slowly fades.

Most of us aren't short on love. We're short on the moments that carry it across. Life gets loud, weeks blur, and the people we'd cross a city for can quietly drift to the edge of our attention — not because we stopped caring, but because care alone doesn't reach them.

Love, But On Purpose exists for that gap. It's a small nudge to turn "I should call them" into "I did." Not as a productivity hack — as a practice. A way of treating love like something you do, on purpose, again and again.

Especially in our twenties, when life is reshuffling fast — friendships need scaffolding, not just sentiment.

The story behind this

It started in a classroom called NST2061.

Love, But On Purpose was inspired by my experience in NST2061, a class where I slowly began to rethink what love actually is. At the start of the semester, I saw love as something largely spontaneous — something that happens naturally if you meet the "right" people. Emotional, intuitive, and somewhat out of our control.

But through the course — especially the discussions on psychological and sociological research — I started to notice a different pattern. Many frameworks suggested that love isn't just a feeling, but something shaped by habits, expectations, and repeated actions. Learning about attachment theory through John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth made me realise that how we show and receive love often follows unconscious patterns formed early in life. Ideas about intentional connection and relationship maintenance challenged my assumption that meaningful relationships simply sustain themselves.

What stood out most was the gap between knowing what matters in relationships and actually acting on it. Many people value their friendships, family, or partners, yet still forget to reach out, check in, or spend time on purpose. Not from a lack of care — from a lack of structure and follow-through.

That gap became the core inspiration for this project. Instead of treating love as something that should naturally fall into place, Love, But On Purpose reframes it as something we can design — through small, intentional actions. Setting a simple connection goal. Receiving a soft reminder. Letting an abstract idea about love become a concrete behaviour.

Ultimately, this project reflects a shift in my own understanding: love is not only something we feel, but something we repeatedly choose to do.

A little context, gently

Relationships grow through small, repeated shared experiences — not grand gestures. The little moments are what actually build closeness over time.

People consistently underestimate how much others appreciate being reached out to. That "they're probably too busy" feeling? It's usually wrong.

Tiny, specific actions are far easier to follow through on than big, vague intentions. "I'll be a better friend" doesn't work. "I'll text Maya on Thursday" does.
